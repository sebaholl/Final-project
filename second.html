The Problem with Plastic

"90% of all ocean plastic comes from 10 rivers."

Ten rivers, mainly in Africa Asia and China, contribute 90% of ocean plastic due to poor waste management near populated areas. Improving these systems can reduce pollution significantly.

"It takes 500+ years for plastic to decompose."

Plastic takes over 500 years to decompose because it is not biodegradable. Instead, it breaks down into smaller fragments called microplastics, which persist in the environment, harming wildlife, ecosystems, and potentially human health.

"1 million marine animals die yearly from plastic."

Plastic pollution has devastating impacts on marine life, with over 1 million seabirds and 100,000 marine animals dying annually due to entanglement or ingestion of plastic debris. Plastic in the ocean breaks down into smaller particles, which are often mistaken for food, leading to fatal health issues for wildlife.

Protecting Vulnerable Communities

Plastic pollution disproportionately affects low-income countries and regions. The treaty proposes legal measures to protect these communities, ensuring their safety from environmental harm (UNEP, 2023)

Promoting Recycling

By improving recycling rates and promoting a circular economy, the treaty encourages efficient resource use, transforming plastic waste into valuable resources and minimizing environmental impact (Ward et al., 2020)

Reducing Plastic Production

The treaty aims to curtail the excessive production and use of plastics, addressing pollution at its source. By promoting alternative materials and eco-friendly product designs, it fosters sustainable consumption (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022)

Plastic pollution is destroying ecosystems, harming wildlife, and contaminating our planet. The Global Plastic Treaty focuses on reducing plastic use, improving recycling, and promoting sustainable alternatives. By cutting single-use plastics and supporting these efforts, we can protect the Earth and build a cleaner, healthier future.
